# The 1D heat equation

The **heat equation** describes how temperature spreads through a thin rod
over time. In one spatial dimension it reads

$$
\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \alpha \, \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2},
\qquad x \in [0, L], \quad t > 0,
$$

where $u(x, t)$ is the temperature and $\alpha > 0$ is the thermal
diffusivity. We pair the equation with an initial condition $u(x, 0) = f(x)$
and (for this chapter) homogeneous Dirichlet boundary conditions
$u(0, t) = u(L, t) = 0$.

## Intuition

The right-hand side, $\partial^2 u / \partial x^2$, is the curvature of the
temperature profile. Where the profile bulges upward, it gets pulled down;
where it dips, it gets pushed up. The bigger $\alpha$ is, the faster that
levelling-out happens.

## Analytical solution by separation of variables

Assume $u(x, t) = X(x) \, T(t)$. Substituting and dividing,

$$
\frac{T'(t)}{\alpha \, T(t)} = \frac{X''(x)}{X(x)} = -\lambda.
$$

The boundary conditions force $X(x) = \sin(n \pi x / L)$ with eigenvalues
$\lambda_n = (n \pi / L)^2$, and then $T_n(t) = e^{-\alpha \lambda_n t}$.
The general solution is

$$
u(x, t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n \sin\!\left(\tfrac{n \pi x}{L}\right) e^{-\alpha (n \pi / L)^2 t},
$$

where the $b_n$ are the Fourier sine coefficients of $f(x)$.

## Numerical solution: explicit FTCS

Discretize $x_i = i \Delta x$ and $t_n = n \Delta t$ and approximate the
derivatives with finite differences. The forward-time, centred-space (FTCS)
scheme is

$$
u_i^{n+1} = u_i^n + r \left( u_{i+1}^n - 2 u_i^n + u_{i-1}^n \right),
\qquad r = \frac{\alpha \, \Delta t}{\Delta x^2}.
$$

This explicit scheme is **stable only when $r \leq 1/2$** &mdash; pick
$\Delta t$ too large and the solution blows up.
